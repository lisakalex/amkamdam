What Is a Crypto Wallet?


Crypto wallets are software programs or hardware devices that allow users to manage, transfer, and store cryptocurrency safely. Some also support NFTs and other digital assets. Wallets can be hardware-based, like USB devices, or software-based, such as mobile apps and desktop programs.

Wallets can also be categorized as custodial or non-custodial:

  • With custodial wallets, the provider controls the private keys, meaning that you rely on them for security. On the flip side, it’s easier to recover access if you lose your login details. Examples include the Binance and OKX wallets.
  • With non-custodial wallets, you have full control of your private key and assets. However, if you lose your private key, you lose access to your funds. Examples include Zengo and NGRAVE.

In other words, custodial wallets offer convenience with some trust in a third party, while non-custodial wallets provide more control and responsibility over your assets.

In essence, a cryptocurrency wallet acts like a digital bank account, managing and safeguarding your cryptocurrency assets.

How Do Crypto Wallets Work?


A cryptocurrency wallet keeps digital assets secure by storing private and public keys, which are used for transactions.

  • Public Key: The public key can be considered the wallet’s “account number” and is used to receive funds from other wallets. It can be shared with others to receive funds.
  • Private Key: The private key is an alphanumeric code unique to each wallet and used to prove ownership of the crypto assets inside. The private key is like a password, meaning it should never be shared with anyone else.

Crypto wallets store and manage the private key – not the actual crypto assets. Crypto assets are held on the blockchain; the private key simply provides access to them and proves ownership.

The best wallets for crypto use advanced cryptographic techniques to protect the user’s private key. Different wallets have different approaches. Examples include encryption, seed phrases, biometric verification, and Hardware Security Modules (HSMs). Digital wallets typically offer backup and recovery options that ensure users can’t lose access to their crypto.

Types of Crypto Wallets: Which Should You Choose?


Deciding to buy cryptocurrency is a huge decision – yet choosing where to store them post-purchase is just as important. There various types of wallets on offer. Below, we go over what software, hardware, and paper wallets are, and which you should choose.

Software Wallets (Hot Wallets)

Software wallets are ideal for frequent traders and users who need easy, quick access to their cryptocurrencies. They offer convenience and accessibility on mobile or desktop devices, making them suitable for everyday transactions, unlike hardware or paper wallets, which are better for long-term storage.

There are two main types of software wallets:

  • Mobile wallets: These are smartphone apps designed to provide on-the-go access to the user’s cryptos. Mobile wallets are often offered for free by top crypto wallets like Best Wallet.
  • Desktop wallets: These are applications that can be downloaded and installed on the user’s computer. Desktop wallets are slightly safer than mobile wallets since they store the user’s private key locally on the computer. MetaMask is an example.

Software wallet are also known as ‘hot wallets’ since they’re connected to the internet. This makes them more susceptible to cyberattacks than ‘cold’ wallets, which have no connection to the internet.

Hardware Wallets (Cold Wallets)

Hardware wallets are best for users prioritizing security, such as long-term investors. They store private keys offline, reducing hacking risks. Ideal for substantial holdings, they offer strong protection against cyber threats, unlike software wallets, while being more durable and user-friendly than paper wallets.

These devices usually look like USB sticks and need to be connected to another device, such as a laptop or smartphone, to initiate transactions. So, despite being safer, hardware wallets aren’t as convenient as software wallets.

Still, they’re essential for investors or traders managing large sums of crypto. In these cases, the relatively high cost of a hardware wallet is justified by the enhanced security it provides, safeguarding significant assets from potential cyber threats.

Paper Wallets

Paper wallets are best for users who need an offline and cost-free storage method. They’re a type of “cold” storage that involves a user writing their public and private keys on a piece of paper.

The user would then store this piece of paper somewhere safe where nobody else could access it — for example, in a safe, safety deposit box, or locker. Ideal for long-term investors, paper wallets avoid online hacking risks. However, they require careful handling to prevent physical loss or damage.

The downside to this approach is that those with paper wallets still eventually need to use a crypto wallet app to spend or transfer digital assets. Thus, paper wallets are relatively impractical and considered less safe than the alternatives. Hence why they aren’t widely used.
